What is it?

Overview

In this task, you will be presented with a sentence containing a word highlighted in yellow followed by questions about that word. We call these words predicates. Predicates refer to some kind of situationâ€”for example, a dynamic event (like running or kicking) or a state of being (like happiness). We're interested in two properties of the situations that different predicates refer to:

  1. whether the predicate describes a dynamic situation(s)
  2. whether the predicate describes a particular situation(s)
We give examples that will help you answer these questions below.

How the task works

The task will begin with a question: The first question is about whether the predicate describes a dynamic situation.

Examples of dynamic situations (click to hide/show)

An example of a predicate describing a dynamic situation can be seen in the following sentence:
Mary is running a half marathon tomorrow.
In this sentence, the predicate running is dynamic, it describes a situation that happened(or is happening, or will happen). Predicates that describe physical activities like running, walking, sleeping are dynamic, and so are mental activities like thinking. Dynamic predicates can appear in various forms. For example the sentence below describes a dynamic situation that takes place instantaneously(unlike running which takes some time):
Mary won the race.
In this sentence, the predicate won is a dynamic event since it again describes a situation that happened. Not all predicates are dynamic, for example:
James owns a car.
The predicate owns does not describe a dynamic situation. Unlike dynamic predicates like running, `own' doesn't describe an event or situation that happened(or is happening, or will happen). It simply describes a state of being - James' ownership of a car. Predicates that describe mental activities can also be non-dynamic. For example:
James believes in god.
In this sentence, the predicate believes doesn't describe a situation that can be considered to happen. Rather, it describes a state of being of James, namely his belief.
The second question is about whether the predicate describes a particular situation or situations.

Examples of particular situations (click to hide/show)

A particular situation is one where it is possible to tell when and where the situation described happened. An example of a predicate referring to a particular situation can be seen in the following example
The lion was eating a zerba.
In this sentence, the predicate eating refers to a particular situation - a certain time in the past where a lion was eating a zebra. Sometimes, the predicate can describe more than one particular situation. An example of this can be seen in the following sentence:
The boysate dinner at their homes.
In this sentence, the predicate ate doesn't refer to one particular situation. Rather it refers to many particular situations of each boy eating dinner at their respective homes. However, not all predicates refer to particular situation or particular situations. Take the following example
Lions eat meat.
The predicate eat doesn't describe a particular situation of eating, nor does it refer to many situations of eating. In this sentence the predicate eat describes a general property of lions.
Sometimes it'll be difficult to tell if the predicate is dynamic, or whether it refers to a particular situation. For this reason, we've provided a drop-down menu to input your confidence about your answer for each of the three questions.
