Relation between two events and their respective durations

Overview

In this task, you will be presented with a sentence containing some color-highlighted words, followed by questions about these words. We call these words predicates. Predicates refer to some kind of situationâ€”for example, a dynamic event (like running or kicking) or a state of being (like happiness or redness).

We are interested in knowing:

  1. the chronology of the situations described by these predicates: did one happen before/after the other?; did they overlap in time?; etc. and
  2. roughly how long the situations described by these predicates likely lasted or will last

How the task works

The task starts with a sentence with some highlighted words within the sentence. For each highlighted predicate, you'll see a slider bar with two sliders on it. The left slider represents the start-point of the situation described the predicate. The right slider represents the end-point of the situation described by the predicate.
The slider bar is shown below. Click and drag the sliders horizontally to change the range.

You will also be asked about the likely total duration of the happened event. We are interested in knowing whether the event took place in seconds, minutes, hours, days, weeks, years, decades, or centuries to happen, or if it's possibly always true.


Click here to reveal/hide examples of duration of an event

Consider a predicate like remodeled.

Sue remodeled her kitchen.

The situation described by remodeled could have taken days, weeks, or months, depending on the job; but the remodeling clearly did not take seconds, nor centuries. You should choose the option that you think is most likely: we would say weeks, but perhaps in your experience you would say months, and that would be an okay response too.

A case of something that would take seconds is the situation described by smiled.

Sue smiled at Jack.

A predicate like danced might describe a situation that takes minutes or even hours to happen.

Sue danced with Jack all evening.

For instance, the situation described by danced in the above sentence took hours.


Some predicates, like tall, can describe situations that hold over decades.

Sue is very tall.

Being tall is not likely to change through most of a person's life, and because people tend to live for decades, we would say this happened over decades.


An example of a situation that takes centuries to happen could be properties of countries, like being largest.

Russia is one of the largest countries in the world.

This will depend on the country in question, along with the other countries in the world, but the situation described by largest likely happens over the course of centuries.


Situations described by some predicates could be true forever.

Three is prime.

This is a mathematical truth, and so it is true forever.

Some predicates have different durations based on different contexts. For example, look at the predicate believe in the below 2 examples.

Jon 1believes in gender equality.
I 2believe no one is coming to office tomorrow.

The situation described by 1believes probably took years or decades to happen but the situation described by 2believe probably just took minutes or hours to happen.

Habits

Ben runs in the park every day.

It's hard to comment how long Ben has been running but it surely looks like a habit of his, so we can comment that the situation described by runs probably took months or years to happen.

Lions roar.

The situation described by roar is true as long as there are lions. So we can comment that this probably took centuries to happen or is true forever.

Note how the same predicate roar can have a different duration depending on the context.
The lion roared at the animal.

The situation described by roared is a specific event and probably just took seconds to happen.
